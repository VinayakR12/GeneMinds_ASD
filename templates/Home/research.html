Research on Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) in India

National Overview

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) affects around 1 in 100 children in India. While awareness is rising in metro cities, rural areas still face significant challenges, including underdiagnosis, lack of services, and societal stigma. Efforts to increase early diagnosis and intervention are needed nationwide.

Regional Variations

  • North India: Growing diagnoses in urban centers, but rural outreach remains insufficient.
  • South India: Strong early intervention programs, especially in Kerala and Karnataka.
  • East India: Limited infrastructure and ongoing traditional beliefs that hinder diagnosis.
  • West India: Cities like Mumbai and Pune lead in research and therapeutic models.
  • Northeast: Extremely low diagnosis rates and an urgent need for targeted government programs.

Causes and Risk Factors

Research suggests a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental factors contributes to ASD. In India, key risk factors include late maternal age, prenatal exposure to infections, poor nutrition, and urban air pollution. Addressing harmful myths about parenting is essential in shifting societal attitudes.

Challenges in Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Scarcity of trained professionals outside major cities.
  • Late identification of ASD cases, missing key early intervention windows.
  • Financial challenges in accessing therapy, particularly due to the high costs and lack of insurance coverage.
  • Social stigma, which delays families from seeking help and support.

Government Initiatives

Autism was officially recognized under the RPWD Act, 2016, granting rights to inclusive education, job reservations, and financial assistance. National Trust schemes and early intervention centers have been introduced to support ASD families. However, accessibility remains uneven, especially in rural areas.

Additional Factors in India

  • Urban vs Rural: Significant gaps in services between urban and rural areas.
  • Gender Gap: Girls are often diagnosed later than boys, with a tendency for underdiagnosis.
  • Technology Aid: Growing use of teletherapy and autism-focused apps offers hope for better access to services.
